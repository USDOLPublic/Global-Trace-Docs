Global Trace

Background

Child labor and forced labor are universally recognized as two of the most critical areas of concern in the production of goods and products. International legal frameworks, as well as national laws, industry certifications and supplier Codes of Conduct implemented by brands and retailers in their responsible sourcing programs, explicitly prohibit child and forced labor. Despite these efforts, child and forced labor continues to exist around the world.

According to the International Labour Organization (ILO), global estimates indicate that 152 million children are subjected to child labor and 24.9 million adults and children to forced labor worldwide. These risks are particularly acute in upstream production activities, such as raw material extraction and agricultural production, which serve as inputs to other industries, and are included in the U.S. Department of Labor List of Goods Produced by Child Labor or Forced Labor, as required under the Trafficking Victims Protection Reauthorization Act (TVPRA). Companies, governments, and other stakeholders agree that tracing supply chains is critical for identifying, addressing, and preventing child and forced labor in supply chains.

In recent years, multiple supply tracing methodologies and tools have been developed and implemented but broad adoption remains limited, partly due to:

  • Complex and rapidly changing supply chains
  • A lack of leveraging extended supply chains, and limited space for information sharing and disclosing upstream supplier information to downstream actors.
  • Data accuracy issues due to implementation of data capture systems without training, oversight or assurance.
  • Limited replicability or scalability of current traceability solutions due to limited number of open source of cost-effective tools.

Technical Solution

Global Trace is a commodity agnostic platform that can be highly configured to enable product traceability of a particular commodity. To do so, there are six configuration steps that are required.

  1. Define products and their associated key data elements (KDEs).
  2. Define key data input roles and assign critical tracking events (CTEs).
  3. Map the supply chain that products will be traced along, indicating the input and output products (defined in Step 1) for each role (defined in Step 2).
  4. Define a taxonomy or list of indicatorsto be used as a framework to combine ESG compliance reports / inputs together.
  5. Upload assessments or self assessment questionnaires (SAQs) for each producer role (defined in Step 2). Within each assessment, questions can be aligned to the list of indicators (defined in Step 4) if their response should contribute to the overall risk associated with a producer.
  6. Specify the method by which various inputs should be combined to determine overall risk associated with a producer. These inputs can include: external risk indices, assessment or SAQ responses (defined in Step 5), compliance reports filed by by ESG assessors (defined in Step 2).

Additional Annexes are also provided, detailing:

  1. How to add external risk indices, that can be used to specify country and commodity level risk.
  2. The process of translating the Global Trace platform into other languages
  3. Purchase and sale configurations of the supply chain
  4. The process used to trace products on Global Trace.
  5. The process of interfacing with Open Supply Hub
  6. Support resources including links to training manuals, training videos, and configuration files for cotton and cobalt pilots.
